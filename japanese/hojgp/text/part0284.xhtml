【ず】

Comes from the auxiliary verb ず, which is used in literary language. Expresses negation. Can be used only in written language and idiomatic expressions. In spoken language, なくて and ないで are used. Created by changing the ない in V-ない to ず. する becomes せず.

1. V-ず

（1）途中と ちゅうであきらめず、最後さい ごまでがんばってください。／Don’t give up in the middle—please keep going until the end.
（2）1時間じ かん待まっても雨あめは止やまず、ぬれて帰かえった。／The rain wouldn’t stop even after I waited for an hour, so I went home drenched.
（3）出発しゅっ ぱつ前日ぜん じつまで予約よ やくが取とれず、心配しん ぱいさせられた。／I couldn’t get a reservation until the day before my departure, so I was really worried.
（4）だれにきいても住所じゅう しょがわからず、困こまった。／No matter who I asked, no one knew the address, so I was in a real fix.
Means the same as V-ないで or V-なくて. (1) is an example of simply putting two clauses side by side to mean “don’t give up.” In many cases, as in (3) and (4), the cause-and-effect relation between the first and second clauses is clear, and V-ず is used as an expression to indicate a reason. Although V-ず is used in spoken language, it is somewhat formal and has the tone of written language.

2. ...ず、...ず

［A-からず、A-からず］

［V-ず、V-ず］

（1）飲のまず食くわずで三日みっか間かんも山中さん ちゅうを歩あるきつづけた。／I continued to walk all over the mountains without eating or drinking for three whole days.
（2）その時とき、彼かれはあわてず騒さわがず一言ひと こと「失礼しつ れいしました」と言いって部屋へ やを出でていった。／At the time, he didn’t get flustered or make a scene, he just left the room with a single remark: “Excuse me.”
（3）展覧会てん らん かいに出品しゅっ ぴんされている作品さく ひんはいずれも負まけず劣おとらずすばらしい。／All the works presented at the exhibition are equally wonderful.
（4）独立どく りつした子供達こ ども たちとは、つかず離はなれずのいい関係かん けいだ。／I have a good relationship with my children, who are now on their own—not too close, but not too far.
（5）日本に ほんの5月がつは暑あつからず、寒さむからずちょうどいい気候き こうです。／The climate in Japan in May is just right—not too hot but not too cold.
（6）客きゃくは多おおからず、少すくなからずほどほどだ。／The number of clients is just right—not too many and not too few.
Means “neither X nor Y.” In some cases words with similar meaning are listed, as in (1), (2), and (3), and in other cases words with contrasting meaning are listed, as in (4), (5), and (6). (3) means that after comparing several items it is found that “all of them are equally wonderful.” (4) means “keeping a suitable distance,” and (5) means “neither hot nor cold.” Idiomatic expressions. Other examples include 鳴かず飛ばず (fall into obscurity), etc.
