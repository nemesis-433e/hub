【ときたら】

1. Nときたら

（1）うちの亭主てい しゅときたら、週しゅうのうち3日かは午前様ご ぜん さまで、日曜にち ようになるとごろごろ寝ねてばかりいる。／That husband of mine—he’s out till after midnight at least three times a week, and then come Sunday, he just lies around the house!
（2）あそこの家いえの中なかときたら、散ちらかし放題ほう だいで足あしの踏ふみ場ばもない。／You know that house over there? The inside is so cluttered that there's no place to stand!
Used by the speaker to introduce a certain person or situation as the topic of conversation and then state an evaluation of that person/situation. The topic is someone or something that is well-known to the speaker, and s/he uses ときたら to express a particularly strong emotion or evaluation that s/he has regarding it. Usually followed by expressions that indicate criticism or dissatisfaction, such as 本当にいやになる (I really hate it) or あきれてしまう (I’m shocked). Used mostly in spoken language.

2. ...ときたら

（1）毎日まい にち残業ざん ぎょうの後あとに飲のみ屋やのはしごときたら、体からだがもつはずがない。／He works overtime every day and then goes bar-hopping, so there's no way his health could hold up.
（2）働はたらき者もので気立き だてがいいときたら、みんなに好すかれるのも無理む りはない。／She’s so hard-working and good-tempered—it's no wonder everyone likes her.
（3）新鮮しん せんな刺さし身みときたら、やっぱり辛口から くちの日本酒に ほん しゅがいいな。／If there’s fresh sashimi (raw fish), I’ll go for—what else?—a dry Japanese sake.
（4）ステーキときたらやっぱり赤あかワインでなくちゃ。／When you’re having steak, you’ve got to have red wine!
Used to introduce as the topic of conversation a person, thing, or situation with an extreme characteristic. Means “in this kind of case/situation, of course this is the only thing to do (or the only thing that will happen).” As shown in (3) and (4), this pattern also takes the form NときたらN, in which case it conveys the meaning that “N goes best with N” or “for N, N is the best.”
